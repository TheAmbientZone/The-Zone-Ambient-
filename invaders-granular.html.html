How to play it

This is a granular synth you play by clearing the board. It's a real instrument wearing an arcade — the game mechanics are the synthesis controls.

The formation is the spectrum

Every invader is a partial of one big drone. Columns are scale degrees (tuned in just intonation), rows are octaves — top row highest, bottom row lowest. A full formation is a thick, rich drone. The grain engine fires tiny slices of your source sound at the pitches of whichever invaders are still alive, so the fuller the board, the denser the spectrum.

Shoot to sculpt

Tap an invader: the cannon fires, it explodes, and that partial drops out of the drone. Carve a wall of sound down to a thin shimmer by picking off invaders — additive synths build up, this one sculpts by subtraction. Tap an empty slot to bring a partial back, or hit new wave to refill the whole spectrum.

The march is the clock

The formation marches and speeds up as invaders die — and that drives a tremolo pulse on the cloud, so as you thin the drone it accelerates. Clearing the board doesn't just quieten it, it changes its rhythm.

Tune vs natural — and why a track sounds like itself

In tune mode the formation re-pitches every grain onto its just-intonation spectrum — so any source becomes the same drone chord, only its timbre changes. That's the signature sound, but it means different tracks can sound alike. Switch to natural and grains keep the track's own pitch, so your songs sound like themselves; the invaders then thin the texture rather than set the harmony. Either way, longer grains keep recognisable pieces of the track instead of a smear — push grain up and a vocal stays a vocal.

Controls

source — load your own sound or use glass / sine / noise / bell. scale — which ratios the columns use (tune mode). grain — slice length, up to 1.5s (short = textural, long = the track speaks). density — grains per second. pitch — transpose. position — where in the sound grains are read from (scrub it). spread — how far across the file grains are scattered. space — reverb. level — output.

The terrain & scanner

The waveform along the bottom is your source. The amber bracket is the scanner — where grains are being read from; its width follows grain size, the faint band is spread. The little white shots rising from it are grains being fired up into the formation. Move position and watch the scanner travel the track.

Tips

To hear a track clearly: natural mode, long grains, low density, move position around. For the choir-drone: tune mode, long grains, high density, then shoot it down to a single held partial. Long grains + very high density is heavy — back off density if it strains. On iPhone open it in Safari, not inside WhatsApp.
